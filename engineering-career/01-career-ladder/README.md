# 01 — Career Ladder

Most engineers have a vague sense of the levels but a poor mental model of what actually differentiates them. This leads to one of the most common and costly mistakes in a software engineering career: doing level N work while hoping for a level N+1 promotion, without understanding that the promotion requires a qualitative shift in how you operate — not just more of the same work done better.

## The core model

Every engineering level can be described along three dimensions:

**Scope** — how large a problem are you expected to own? A function? A service? A system? A domain?

**Autonomy** — how much supervision do you need? Assigned tasks with check-ins? Independent execution? Self-directed problem identification?

**Judgment** — how often do you make consequential calls without a playbook? How good are those calls?

These three dimensions compound. A senior engineer is not a mid engineer who writes more code. A senior engineer owns a larger scope, operates with more autonomy, and makes more judgment calls per week. Demonstrating that you already operate that way is what actually triggers a promotion — not tenure, not task completion, not being liked.

## The two qualitative jumps

Most of the standard level progression is a gradual increase. Two transitions are qualitatively different:

**Mid → Senior**: This jump requires shifting from "I execute the problems I am assigned" to "I identify and define problems, not just solve them." A mid engineer makes the assigned work go well. A senior engineer decides what work matters, pushes back on the wrong work, and spots the problem that hasn't been named yet. This is why many technically strong engineers stall at mid: they are executing well but not leading a scope.

**Senior → Staff**: This jump requires shifting from "I own my technical area" to "I influence areas I do not directly control." A staff engineer does not just make their team's systems better — they move the technical thinking of people and teams who don't report to them, resolve architectural ambiguity at the organizational level, and set direction that outlasts any single project.

Understanding these jumps tells you what to invest in. If you want to get from mid to senior, the investment is not more coding — it is scoping, ownership, and communication.
