# Levels and Expectations

Engineering levels are not primarily about years of experience, number of systems shipped, or technical depth. They are about the combination of **scope**, **autonomy**, and **judgment** an engineer is expected to operate at.

Understanding this changes how you think about your own career. Instead of waiting to be recognized, you can look at the level above you, understand what it requires, and deliberately start operating at that level before you're formally in it.

## The level map

### Software Analyst / Junior Engineer

**Scope**: assigned tasks within a defined feature or component. You are not expected to identify the problem — only to solve the one you've been given.

**Autonomy**: supervised. Your code is reviewed closely, your designs are guided, and your estimates are checked. This is not a judgment on you; it's appropriate for where you are.

**Judgment**: you are learning what good looks like. You should be asking questions more than making independent calls.

**What success looks like**: you complete assigned work reliably, ask good questions, and learn from code review without getting defensive. You are building familiarity with the codebase, the domain, and the team's standards.

**The common failure mode**: treating the job as "do what I'm told" without investing in understanding *why* things are done a certain way. Execution without curiosity does not build the judgment you'll need at the next level.

---

### Mid-Level Engineer

**Scope**: a feature or service. You own the full implementation of a piece of work, including the edge cases and the tests, without needing step-by-step guidance.

**Autonomy**: largely independent on well-defined problems. You should be able to take a ticket or a feature brief and execute it without hand-holding. You still need guidance on ambiguous or high-stakes decisions.

**Judgment**: you make most implementation decisions yourself. You can choose between two approaches and explain the trade-off. You escalate the right things and don't escalate the wrong things.

**What success looks like**: your work ships reliably and doesn't create regressions. You improve things around your assigned work without being asked. You contribute to design discussions with real substance, not just questions.

**The common failure mode**: being technically solid but waiting to be told what to do. A mid engineer who only executes assigned work without identifying problems, proposing improvements, or owning a wider scope will stay mid for a long time.

---

### Senior Engineer

**Scope**: a technical area or system. You own not just implementation but the health, direction, and trade-offs of a domain. You identify problems that haven't been named yet, not just solve the ones assigned.

**Autonomy**: self-directed on most problems. You decide what to work on, why, and how. You align with the team and manager but don't need permission for most technical calls.

**Judgment**: you make consequential calls regularly. You can reason through trade-offs in new situations. When you don't know the answer, you know how to find it and aren't afraid to say "I don't know yet."

**What success looks like**: your team moves faster because of you. You unblock others, identify the right things to build, push back on the wrong things, and make architectural decisions that hold up. You make other engineers better through code review, design feedback, and mentoring.

**The common failure mode**: doing senior-level technical work but communicating like a mid. Writing great code in isolation, not sharing context, not writing design docs, not speaking up in planning. Technical excellence without communication is invisible.

---

### Staff Engineer

**Scope**: cross-team or cross-domain. You own technical direction and quality for an area that spans multiple teams. You resolve architectural ambiguity that no individual team can resolve alone.

**Autonomy**: fully self-directed. You identify problems at the organizational level and propose solutions. Your calendar is largely self-managed around the technical problems that matter most.

**Judgment**: you operate at a meta-level — deciding which problems to solve, not just how to solve them. You identify the right sequencing and trade-offs across systems and time horizons.

**What success looks like**: multiple teams are more aligned and more effective because of your influence. You write documents that change how teams think. You raise the technical bar by setting standards, not just enforcing them.

---

## The promotion mechanism

Promotions happen when your manager and their manager have enough evidence that you are already operating at the next level consistently. The key word is **evidence**.

Evidence is visible. It comes from:

- things you wrote (design docs, RFCs, post-mortems, code reviews)
- things you said in public forums (design reviews, planning meetings, incident calls)
- things that shipped or improved because of your judgment
- feedback from teammates who were unblocked or made better by your involvement

Evidence is not your private contribution to good code that only the reviewer sees. If your senior-level work is happening entirely in pull request diffs and private conversations, your manager will not have enough material for the promotion case. This is not unfair; it is the system. Your job is to generate legible evidence.

## The practical implication

For every level transition you want to make, ask: "What does someone at the next level do that I am not doing yet?" Then start doing those things — not to perform, but because operating at that level is genuinely what gets the work done better. The promotion follows from the behavior, not the other way around.
