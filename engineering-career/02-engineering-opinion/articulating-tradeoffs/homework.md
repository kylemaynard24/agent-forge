# Homework — Articulating Trade-offs

## Exercise 1: The full structure

Pick a technical decision from your current work or a recent one — it does not need to be a big one. Write a structured trade-off analysis using the five-part structure from the README:

1. **Context**: what is the specific situation and what constraints make this non-obvious?
2. **Options**: list at least two genuinely viable options
3. **Dimensions**: what are the 3-5 dimensions that matter most for this decision?
4. **Recommendation**: what do you recommend and why? Connect the recommendation to the dimensions explicitly.
5. **Caveats**: what would change your recommendation?

Do this for a decision that has already been made. The goal is to practice the structure, not to reopen the decision.

## Exercise 2: Diagnose a weak trade-off analysis

Write out a brief "it depends" answer to a technical question — either from memory (a time someone gave you a vague answer in a meeting) or invented. Something like: "Should we use a monolith or microservices? It depends on the team size and the requirements."

Then rewrite it as a complete trade-off analysis using the structure above, applied to a specific fictional or real scenario.

Compare the two versions. What was missing from the first? What does the second communicate that the first doesn't?

## Exercise 3: The verbal version

Find a friend, colleague, or AI (yes, you can use Claude) and practice delivering a trade-off analysis verbally for a technical question. Pick a real question you have an opinion on — something from your current work, from the architecture curriculum, or from the agentic workflows curriculum.

Deliver the analysis in under 3 minutes, hitting all five parts.

After the practice:
1. Did you stay on topic or drift?
2. Did you actually make a recommendation or hedge at the end?
3. What was the weakest part of your analysis?

## Exercise 4: Audit a recent meeting

Think about the last design discussion or technical decision meeting you attended. For any decision that was made:

1. Were all the viable options identified?
2. Were the relevant dimensions named?
3. Was a recommendation made and was it grounded in the dimensions?
4. If the discussion was weak — lots of "it depends," lots of circular talk, no clear recommendation — what was missing?

You do not need to have spoken in the meeting to do this analysis. You're building the pattern recognition for good versus weak trade-off articulation so you can apply it when you're the one speaking.
