# Homework — What Interviews Actually Test

## Exercise 1: Signal mapping

For each of the three main interview types below, write what signals the interviewer is trying to collect. Be specific — not just "can they code" but "can they break a problem down before writing any code? can they handle being told their first approach won't work?"

1. **Coding interview**: what 3-5 specific signals is the interviewer collecting?
2. **System design interview**: what 3-5 specific signals?
3. **Behavioral interview**: what 3-5 specific signals?

This is not a recall exercise. Think about it from the interviewer's perspective: if you had 45 minutes to decide whether to hire someone, what would you be trying to learn?

## Exercise 2: Self-diagnosis

Think about a past interview (or a mock interview you do for this exercise) where you felt it went poorly. Diagnose it using the failure modes from the README:

1. Did you work silently and only present answers? (No process visible)
2. Did you respond defensively to any feedback or pushback?
3. Did you clarify requirements before jumping in?
4. Did you acknowledge any knowledge gaps honestly?
5. Were you trying to perform or demonstrate?

If you have not done a real or mock interview recently, do one: ask a colleague to ask you a technical question and talk through it out loud. Then self-diagnose.

## Exercise 3: Practice thinking out loud

Pick any problem — a coding problem, a system design prompt, a technical question — and spend 15 minutes working through it out loud. Not writing, not thinking silently. Out loud.

After:
1. Was it uncomfortable? If so, why?
2. Did you catch yourself going silent? How many times?
3. Did you ask clarifying questions before starting?
4. Did your verbal reasoning match what you were actually thinking?

The discomfort of thinking out loud is exactly what this exercise is training away. Repeat it until it feels normal.

## Exercise 4: Interview from the other side

If you have the opportunity, do a mock interview where you are the interviewer for someone else (a colleague, a friend learning to code). Ask a technical question and evaluate the signals.

After:
1. What did you find yourself paying attention to?
2. What behavior made you think "this person is strong" regardless of whether the answer was right?
3. What behavior made you unsure, regardless of whether the answer was right?

Seeing what it looks like from the evaluator's perspective is one of the fastest ways to change your behavior as a candidate.
