# 05 — Market and Positioning

Most engineers approach their career financially the way most people approach buying a car: they take what they're offered because they don't know what the real number is or how the negotiation works. The result is systematic undercompensation that compounds over years, since future offers and raises are often anchored to the previous number.

Market and positioning intelligence doesn't require becoming a mercenary or optimizing exclusively for money. It requires knowing enough about how the market works to make decisions from information rather than from uncertainty.

This section covers two topics:

| Topic | What it answers |
| --- | --- |
| [`market-awareness/`](market-awareness/) | How engineering compensation works, where to find real market data, when and how leverage arises, and how to use it without blowing up relationships |
| [`evaluating-opportunities/`](evaluating-opportunities/) | How to assess companies, teams, and roles — not just by brand or comp but by the dimensions that actually predict whether the opportunity will accelerate your career |

## The market reality

Software engineering compensation in the United States (and increasingly globally) has a wide distribution. At any given level, the spread between a well-positioned engineer at a company with strong equity and one at an underpaying company can be 2-4x total compensation. This is not primarily a skill gap. It is an information and positioning gap.

The market does not correct this automatically. Employers benefit from information asymmetry and do not volunteer it. The responsibility is entirely on the individual engineer to know what the market actually pays, what their leverage is, and when to act on it.

## When this matters most

This section is not primarily about gaming the system. It is about:

1. **Not leaving large amounts of money on the table** by accepting offers without negotiating or staying too long without reassessing
2. **Making move decisions from data**, not from "I don't know if I can do better"
3. **Evaluating opportunities accurately**, so that you take the one that actually advances your career — not just the one with the highest base salary
4. **Understanding your leverage** so that when you have it, you know to use it, and when you don't, you know what to do to get it

The best time to understand this material is before you need it, not during an active job search or offer negotiation.
