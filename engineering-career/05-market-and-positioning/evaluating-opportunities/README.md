# Evaluating Opportunities

Most engineers evaluate job opportunities on the wrong dimensions. Brand name, total compensation, and perks are easy to compare and get most of the attention. The dimensions that actually predict whether you will grow, do your best work, and advance your career get far less scrutiny.

The result is that people take impressive-looking jobs that stall their development, or turn down opportunities that would have been transformative, because they were optimizing for the visible dimension.

This is a framework for evaluating opportunities on the dimensions that actually matter.

## The dimensions that actually predict career growth

### 1. Manager quality

Your manager is the most important variable in your day-to-day experience and your career trajectory at a company. A strong manager will advocate for you, give you real feedback, create opportunities for you to do higher-leverage work, and help you navigate the organization. A weak manager will create friction, block opportunities, and leave you invisible.

Manager quality is also something you can assess in the interview process, which most candidates don't realize.

Questions to ask the hiring manager directly:

- "Can you tell me about two or three engineers who were on your team a year ago and where they are now?" (Looking for growth — promotions, expanded scope, moved to higher-impact roles — not for "they're still here.")
- "How do you handle performance feedback? What's an example of difficult feedback you've given recently?" (Looking for evidence that they give real feedback, not just praise.)
- "What's something about how this team operates that you'd change if you could?" (Looking for self-awareness and honesty.)
- "What does a great quarter look like for someone in this role?"

If the answers are vague, scripted, or all positive with no nuance, treat that as a signal.

### 2. Team quality

You will learn more from the engineers around you than from any course or curriculum. A team with three strong, opinionated senior engineers who give real code review will develop you faster than a team where you are the best engineer and receive no challenge.

Questions to ask when you meet the team:

- "What's the most interesting technical problem you've worked on in the last six months?"
- "What parts of the codebase or system are you most proud of? What parts are you least proud of?" (Looking for self-awareness and honesty.)
- "What does code review culture look like here? How thorough is it?"

Pay attention to whether the engineers you meet seem sharp, curious, and willing to be direct. Pay attention to whether the conversation feels substantive or performative.

### 3. Scope and ownership

Will you own something meaningfully? Or will you be executing tickets in a small slice of a system that a handful of more senior people actually control?

This matters because ownership is how you develop judgment and gather the evidence for promotion. Working on things where you are the subject-matter expert, make the calls, and own the outcome — even at small scope — teaches you more than executing in a well-defined lane.

Questions to ask:

- "What would I own in the first six months? What decisions would be mine to make?"
- "Who are the primary decision-makers for technical direction in this area?"
- "What does the path to taking on a larger ownership area look like?"

### 4. Technical health of the codebase

The condition of the codebase you'll be working in affects almost everything: your ability to ship confidently, the cognitive load of your daily work, how much of your time is spent on the problem versus the accidental complexity.

You cannot fully assess this before joining, but you can get meaningful signal:

- Ask to see a representative pull request or code review. If they say no, that is signal.
- Ask how long the average PR takes from open to merge. Very long cycles often indicate heavy bureaucracy or significant review culture problems.
- Ask "what is your biggest area of technical debt and how are you managing it?" An honest answer is encouraging. "We don't really have technical debt" is a red flag.
- Ask about test coverage and deployment process. How often do they deploy? How automated is it? What's the release process? These are proxies for engineering maturity.

### 5. Company and team trajectory

Are you joining a team that is growing in scope and investment, or one that is shrinking? A team that is gaining influence within the organization creates opportunities; one that is losing influence or being deprioritized limits them.

Questions to ask:

- "How has the headcount and scope of this team changed in the last two years?"
- "What's the investment direction for this area over the next 12-18 months?"
- "How does this team's work connect to the company's most important priorities right now?"

Tread carefully here — companies sometimes spin stories about their most challenged teams to recruit talent. Cross-check by researching public signals (product direction, job postings, press) and by asking questions whose honest answers would be unflattering, to see whether you get honest answers.

### 6. Culture around growth and learning

Will you be pushed to grow? Will you be given stretch assignments? Is mentorship and technical growth something the organization actually invests in, or something they put in the career page but not the work?

Questions to ask:

- "What's an example of how the company invested in someone's growth recently?"
- "What's your expectation for senior engineers around mentoring and developing more junior teammates?"
- "Are there ways engineers here publish, speak, or contribute externally?"

## Red flags that are hard to see from outside

**Vague or inconsistent answers about ownership**: if two different people describe the team's scope differently, or if ownership descriptions are vague, it suggests the organization is unclear about who owns what. This leads to conflict and political navigation that is exhausting and non-developmental.

**No clear path from here**: if you ask "what's the path from this role to the next level?" and get a vague answer, that means either the company does not have a clear engineering ladder or no one in the room has thought about it for this role. Both are concerning.

**Everyone sounds too happy**: engineers who work at good companies can describe what is genuinely difficult or frustrating about their situation. If everyone you meet sounds like they are reading from a recruiting script, something is being managed.

**High attrition in recent history**: ask how many people from the team from a year ago are still there. If significant turnover is explained as "people outgrew the company" or "we had some unfortunate departures," probe further.

**Urgency or pressure to decide quickly**: a common tactic to close candidates before they have time to do due diligence. A company that respects you will give you reasonable time to evaluate a decision of this magnitude.

## The practical evaluation process

When evaluating an opportunity, structure your assessment explicitly:

1. After each interview conversation, write down your read on the dimension being demonstrated (manager quality, team quality, etc.)
2. After the process, score each dimension: strong positive, neutral, concern, or red flag
3. Identify which dimensions you have incomplete information on and decide whether to surface specific questions before deciding
4. Make the decision based on the full picture, weighted toward the dimensions that most affect long-term career health

Total compensation matters. But a 15% higher base salary at a company where you stall for three years costs you much more in lost career development than the money gained. The engineers who have the most leverage five years from now are the ones who spent those five years in environments that challenged them and gave them real ownership.
