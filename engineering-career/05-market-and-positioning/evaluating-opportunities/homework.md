# Homework — Evaluating Opportunities

## Exercise 1: Scorecard for a real or hypothetical opportunity

Either for a job you are currently considering or for a hypothetical one (a company and role you would be interested in), build a scorecard:

For each dimension, write at least one sentence of evidence that supports your score. Not "seems okay" — what specifically did you learn that informs this?

Then answer: which dimensions are you most uncertain about? What question would you need to ask to reduce that uncertainty?

## Exercise 2: Question bank

Write out five questions you would ask in each of the following interview contexts to get real, useful information:

1. **Questions for the hiring manager** (about their management style, team growth, feedback culture)
2. **Questions for potential teammates** (about technical culture, codebase health, collaboration)
3. **Questions about the company** (trajectory, investment in this area, prioritization)

These should be questions that would be uncomfortable for a bad situation to answer honestly but easy for a good situation to answer. "Is this a good team?" is not such a question. "Can you tell me about someone on this team from a year ago and where they are now?" is.

## Exercise 3: Red flag review

Read the red flags section of the README. For the last role you considered or accepted, answer:

1. Which of these red flags were present?
2. Did you notice them at the time? Why or why not?
3. If you did notice them and took the role anyway, why? Was that the right call?
4. What would you do differently when evaluating the next opportunity?

## Exercise 4: Retrospective on a past decision

Think about a job change (or a decision to stay put) that you made in the last 2-3 years. Evaluate it against the dimensions in the README:

1. Which dimensions drove your decision at the time?
2. Which dimensions turned out to matter most once you were in the role?
3. What did you over-weight at the time (brand? comp? title)?
4. What did you under-weight?
5. What one piece of information, if you had known it before deciding, would have most changed your decision?

The goal of this exercise is not regret but calibration. Understanding what you over- and under-weighted in the past makes you a better evaluator the next time.
