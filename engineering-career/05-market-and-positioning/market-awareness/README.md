# Market Awareness

Most engineers systematically underprice themselves. Not because they lack ability, but because they have no market data and because the systems they are inside (their current employer, their annual review cycle, their manager's budget) have incentives that do not align with getting them to market rate.

Market awareness is the practice of understanding what the market actually pays for your skills, level, and location — so that you can make decisions from information rather than from uncertainty.

## How engineering compensation is structured

At most tech companies, total compensation (TC) has three components:

**Base salary**: your cash salary, paid regularly. The most visible and most discussed number. Not the most important number at most larger companies.

**Bonus**: a cash amount paid annually or semi-annually, often tied to company or individual performance targets. Ranges from 5% of base (common at smaller companies) to 20-30%+ (common at larger companies).

**Equity**: stock, either restricted stock units (RSUs) or options. At public companies, RSUs vest over time (typically 4 years with a 1-year cliff) and are worth real money. At private companies, the value is theoretical until a liquidity event. Equity can represent a large fraction of total compensation — at some large tech companies, it exceeds base salary at senior levels.

The relative weighting matters enormously when comparing offers. A $180k base with no equity and a small bonus can be less total compensation than a $150k base with $100k in annual RSU vesting and a 15% bonus target. Do the math for year 1 and year 4, because equity vesting changes the picture over time.

## Where to find real compensation data

The single biggest reason engineers underprice themselves is that they do not have market data. Fixing this takes 30 minutes.

**levels.fyi**: the most comprehensive database of engineering compensation by company, level, and location. Self-reported by engineers, covers most major tech companies. Filter by company + level + location to get accurate comps for your specific target.

**Glassdoor**: broader coverage than levels.fyi but less detailed for senior levels. Better for companies not well-represented on levels.fyi.

**Blind**: an anonymous professional network where engineers share offer details and discuss compensation openly. Especially useful for getting a sense of what a specific team or role actually pays, beyond the official ranges.

**Your network**: if you have peers who have changed jobs recently, their offer data is the highest-quality market signal you can get. More engineers share this than you might expect if you ask directly and frame it as mutual exchange.

**Recruiter conversations**: even if you're not actively searching, taking recruiter calls and letting them get to the point where they share a range gives you free market data. You don't owe them anything. You're just getting information.

## The leverage principle

You have negotiating leverage when you have alternatives. The strongest position in any compensation conversation is "I have an offer from another company." Without that, you are negotiating against a company that knows your alternative is "nothing" or "uncertainty," which is structurally weak.

This is why the most effective way to ensure you are paid at market rate is to periodically re-enter the market — not necessarily to leave, but to generate competing offers. You learn your real market value, you generate leverage if you decide to use it, and you make the implicit threat of leaving credible without having to state it.

Engineers who stay at one company for many years and never interview elsewhere are almost always below market rate, because:

- salary increases internally are typically 2-5% annually
- market rates for engineers with experience compound faster than internal raises
- new hire offers at the same company often exceed what you're being paid for the same level

The gap compounds. An engineer who was paid market rate at hire and has been getting 3% raises for 5 years is often 20-40% below market rate for their current level.

## How negotiation actually works

Most engineers do not negotiate their first offer or negotiate weakly. This is the single most expensive negotiation mistake, because the initial number often anchors future raises and offers.

The basic principles:

**Never be the first to name a number.** If a recruiter asks "what are you looking for?" the best response is "I'm more interested in understanding the full scope of the role and the band for this level. Can you share the range?" Most recruiters will share the range at this point. If they push back, say "I'll consider any offer at the competitive market rate for this level."

**Name a number above what you'd accept.** If you are forced to name a number, add 10-15% to what you'd actually take. You are starting a negotiation, not a confession.

**Counter every first offer.** First offers are almost never final offers. A professional counter that says "I'm very excited about this role. Based on my research, the market rate for this level is X. Can you get to Y?" is standard practice. Most companies expect it.

**Use competing offers explicitly.** "I have an offer from Company B at X. I prefer Company A — can you match it?" This is the most effective form of negotiation because it converts your ask from "I want more" to "here is an objective market data point."

**Get everything in writing before accepting.** Base, equity (including grant size, vesting schedule, and refresh grants), bonus target, benefits, and start date.

## When your leverage is highest

Your leverage is highest in specific windows:

- When you have a competing offer
- When you have just been promoted (your internal value just increased; market rate should reflect that)
- When the company is hiring aggressively for your skills
- When you have just shipped something significant and have a track record of impact

Your leverage is lowest when you are under performance management, when the company is cutting headcount, or when you have no alternatives lined up.

The practical implication: do not wait until you are unhappy to build your negotiating position. The time to interview elsewhere is before you desperately need to leave. The time to understand your market rate is before you're in an offer conversation. Preparation always happens before the moment.
