# Homework — Market Awareness

## Exercise 1: Build a market data picture

Spend 30 minutes collecting real compensation data for your current level, tech stack, and location (or remote-friendly equivalent).

Sources to use:
- levels.fyi: filter by level + location + relevant companies
- Glassdoor: search your role + location
- One conversation with a peer who has changed jobs in the last 12 months

Write down:
1. The range you found for your level and skills
2. Where you fall in that range (approximate is fine)
3. If you are below the midpoint — by how much, and for how long have you been below it?

If you genuinely do not know your current total compensation including equity, calculate it. Base + expected bonus + annual equity vesting (RSU grant value divided by vesting period). This number is frequently much higher or lower than people think.

## Exercise 2: Total comp calculation

For your current role, calculate your actual total compensation:

1. Base salary
2. Expected bonus (percent target × base)
3. Annual equity value (total grant ÷ vesting years, or annual RSU grant value)
4. Other meaningful benefits (employer health contribution, 401k match, etc.)

Add them up. Compare to the market range you found in Exercise 1.

If this exercise is uncomfortable, that discomfort is signal.

## Exercise 3: Negotiation rehearsal

Write out how you would respond to the following situation: a recruiter has contacted you about a role that sounds interesting. They ask, "What are you currently making and what are you looking for?"

Write out:
1. Your actual response (what would you say?)
2. Your ideal response (what should you say, using the principles from the README?)
3. What is the gap between the two?

If your actual response involves immediately sharing your current salary, practice the alternative: "I'm flexible and focused on the total picture. Can you share the band for this role?"

## Exercise 4: Leverage inventory

Answer honestly:

1. When was the last time you had a competing offer or a concrete alternative?
2. When is the last time you interviewed externally, even without intending to leave?
3. What would your negotiating position look like today if you wanted to renegotiate your compensation?
4. What would need to be true to have a stronger position in 6 months?

The goal is not to make you anxious about your career. It is to make the question "what is my leverage right now?" a thing you know the answer to, rather than something that surprises you when you need it.

## Clean Code Lens

**Principle in focus:** Named evidence; explicit reasoning chains

A compensation negotiation backed by "I feel like I'm worth more" is an assertion without evidence — it fails the same way an undocumented architectural decision fails. A negotiation backed by "Levels.fyi shows the P50 for my level and stack at $X in my metro; I am at $Y, which is 18% below market; I have two named recent accomplishments that exceed my level's expectations" is structured communication with named sources, named comparisons, and named conclusions that are auditable and persuasive.

**Exercise:** Write your negotiation case as if it were a structured argument: name your data sources, name the specific figures, name the gap, and name the evidence from your own work that justifies closing it — then remove any sentence that contains "I think" or "I feel" without a named fact behind it.

**Reflection:** If your manager asked you to justify your compensation request in a three-minute summary, would your preparation produce a structured argument with named evidence — or would you be assembling it on the spot?
