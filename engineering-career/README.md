# engineering-career

This section is the professional dimension of the `agent-forge` curriculum — the part that answers not "how do I build good software?" but "how do I build a good engineering career?"

Technical excellence is necessary but not sufficient. Engineers who are technically strong but professionally inert get passed over, underpaid, and stuck. Engineers who develop both — the craft and the career intelligence — move with agency.

This section covers five areas:

| Area | What it teaches |
| --- | --- |
| [`01-career-ladder/`](01-career-ladder/) | How engineering levels work, what differentiates each level, and the specific path from analyst to senior and beyond |
| [`02-engineering-opinion/`](02-engineering-opinion/) | How to form, articulate, and defend technical opinions — including writing design docs, presenting trade-offs, and having principled disagreements |
| [`03-technical-interviews/`](03-technical-interviews/) | What interviews actually measure, how to approach system design and behavioral interviews, and how to prepare deliberately |
| [`04-engineering-judgment/`](04-engineering-judgment/) | How engineering taste develops, how to learn from production, and how to build judgment that goes beyond following rules |
| [`05-market-and-positioning/`](05-market-and-positioning/) | Market awareness, compensation mechanics, and how to evaluate opportunities so you're making informed decisions, not reactive ones |

## The through-line

All five areas are about the same underlying thing: **reducing dependency on external conditions for your career forward motion**.

- Dependency on a specific manager to sponsor you → replaced by knowing what each level requires and demonstrating it visibly
- Dependency on whoever is willing to hire you → replaced by being able to perform well in the interview process deliberately
- Dependency on whatever salary you were offered → replaced by knowing market rates and the mechanics of leverage
- Dependency on being "discovered" for your next opportunity → replaced by knowing how to position yourself and evaluate options critically
- Dependency on consensus for technical decisions → replaced by being able to form grounded opinions and articulate them under pressure

Career agency is not charisma or politics. It is the combination of technical credibility and professional intelligence.

## How this section connects to the rest of the repo

The technical curriculum in `software-engineering/` and `agentic-workflows/` builds the foundation that career agency rests on. You cannot have a credible engineering opinion without genuine understanding. You cannot perform well in a system design interview without having actually studied system design. You cannot navigate the senior level without the debugging, performance, security, and incident judgment that `software-engineering/advanced-engineering/` trains.

The sequence is: build the foundation → build career intelligence on top of it. This section assumes you are doing both in parallel.

## How to study this section

Most topics here do not have runnable demos. They have:

- **README.md** — the concept explained in plain language with enough depth to act on
- **homework.md** — application exercises that force you to do the thing, not just read about it

The homework in this section is often written exercises: drafting a design doc, writing out a behavioral story, articulating a trade-off analysis. That is intentional. These skills are built through practice in the medium where they are actually used — writing, conversation, and deliberate reflection.
